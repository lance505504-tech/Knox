Where Things Stand

The US Situation

The Cape31 has been in the US since the early days of the class. It arrived first in New Orleans, quickly proved itself in IRC and ORC fleets, and has been winning everywhere it races. But here’s the thing — it’s still doing that as scattered individual boats competing in mixed ORC fleets. The full potential of the class — pure one-design, boat-for-boat racing — is still untapped in the US.

With 11 boats now on both coasts and more deliveries planned, 2025 and 2026 are the years to build the one-design clusters that make this class what it is in the UK, the Mediterranean and South Africa. The infrastructure of the US racing calendar is already perfect for it — Key West in winter, Charleston and Newport in spring and summer, San Francisco in September.

The model is simple: build a local fleet of 6-8 boats and you have world-class one-design racing on your home waters. The US is big enough for two distinct fleets — East Coast and West Coast — that meet once a year at the Gold Cup in Saint-Tropez. And because the boat fits in a container, owners can chase the season across both coasts or head to the UK or the Med when they want an international experience.

Sandy Askew from Salt Lake City did exactly that — took her boat to the UK to get one-design experience, won back-to-back UK National Championships and the Britannia Cup at Cowes Week. That’s what happens when an American owner commits to this class.

🌞

West Coast

San Francisco Bay is the natural home for the Cape31 on the West Coast. Consistent 18-25 knot westerlies, world-class venues at St Francis YC and GGYC, and a deeply competitive ORC fleet that the C31 already dominates. Pacific Yankee and Warrior Won have been racing each other all summer long. The 61st Rolex Big Boat Series in September is already listed as a Cape31 one-design class event.

East Coast

Newport, Long Island Sound, Annapolis, Charleston, Key West — the full circuit of US East Coast sailing is perfectly suited to the C31. Cool Breeze races at NYYC events out of Newport. The Key West circuit in January is the obvious winter gathering point. Charleston Race Week is one of the biggest regattas in the country. The boats are already there — they just need more of them.

The Opportunity

Six boats in one location = one-design racing. The US fleet is almost there on both coasts. Two or three new owners on each coast tips the balance from “handful of boats in ORC” to “proper fleet.” The container/trailer flexibility means you can base the boat anywhere and move it for key events. Southernmost Regatta (Key West), Charleston Race Week, Newport Summer and the Rolex Big Boat Series form a natural national calendar.

The Proof of Concept

Sandy Askew’s Story

What happens when an American owner fully commits to the Cape31. She went to the UK to race one-design. She won everything.

Salt Lake City to Cowes Week

Sandy Askew — Salt Lake City, Utah — came to the Cape31 already racing a Melges 24, a VX One and an IC37. She knew performance one-design sailing. When she got in a C31, she recognised something special immediately.

Unable to find enough one-design racing in the US, she shipped her boat to the UK. Flying Jenny raced in the UK Cape31 circuit, competing against the best C31 sailors in the world at events including the UK Nationals and Cowes Week.

The results speak for themselves: back-to-back UK National Championships in 2024 and 2025 — the only owner ever to win consecutive titles. And then, at Cowes Week 2025, Flying Jenny won the Britannia Cup — beating 23 higher-rated IRC yachts at the most famous sailing regatta in the world.

Sandy’s story is both an inspiration and a blueprint. The C31 rewards commitment at the very highest level. Now the question for the US fleet is: when do we build the one-design racing here so American owners don’t have to ship to England to get it?

Six Reasons to Get Involved

Why Cape31 in the US

01

Already Winning

Rolex at Big Boat Series. 1-2-3 at NYYC Annual. 1-2 at Safe Harbour Race Week. The C31 isn’t a new class in the US trying to prove itself — it’s already the fastest, most successful boat in its class at every major event it enters. You’re joining a winner.

02

Smashes ORC & IRC

The C31 is the only 31-footer winning against boats up to 40 feet in ORC and IRC. Race one-design AND beat the big boats on rating. San Francisco Bay, Newport, Annapolis — it’s cleaning up coast to coast. You don’t have to choose between class racing and mixed fleet success.

03

Perfect for US Conditions

SF Bay at 20-25 knots westerly. Block Island Race Week chop. Charleston breeze. Key West January sunshine. The C31 was built for exactly these conditions — bow up, 20 knots downwind, rock solid upwind. Drew Freides put it best: “It’s an amazing boat for the Bay.”

04

Container & Trailer

The boat fits on a standard trailer and in a standard 40ft container. Base it in Newport, trailer to Annapolis for the fall, ship to the Med for the Gold Cup. American sailors already move boats for Key West. The C31 is designed to follow the wind and the racing, coast to coast and continent to continent.

05

Owner-Driver. Not an Arms Race.

Strict one-design. Maximum three professionals. All hulls, rigs and keels unmodified. Racing decided by sailor skill, tactics and teamwork — not budget. The class actively polices this ethos. Sandy Askew won back-to-back UK Nationals as owner-driver. Your boat, your rules, your crew.

06

International Class

80+ boats across 7 regions. The Gold Cup in Saint-Tropez every October. UK fleet at Cowes Week. Med Circuit across five countries. Join the US fleet and you’re automatically part of an international class that races at the world’s best venues. US boats travel to the Gold Cup and UK boats come to the Rolex Big Boat Series.
